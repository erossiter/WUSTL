Medicare Part D   Contact  Barbara Riley      Sunday  August 5  2007      WASHINGTON  DC  Citing medication safety concerns and the potential for savings  United States Senator John Sununu  R NH  reintroduced legislation to provide financial incentives  without increasing federal costs  to physicians who write electronic prescriptions or  e scripts  for medications covered under the Medicare Part D Prescription Drug Program  Sununus Electronic Prescription Drug Act of 2007 would reward doctors who write electronic prescriptions by reducing the amount Medicare pays pharmacies to fill handwritten prescriptions   Although jokes about physician handwriting may be common  patient safety is no laughing matter  With roughly 8 8 million preventable adverse prescription drug related incidents occurring each year  it is time for physicians to rapidly move toward writing electronic prescriptions  said Sununu  who noted that computer generated prescriptions have been shown to reduce errors by about 70  over hand written prescriptions  The Institute of Medicine has recognized e prescribing as one of the most promising tools to reduce adverse drug related errors and has recommended that all prescriptions be written electronically by 2010  The Electronic Prescription Drug Act of 2007 takes an important step towards meeting this benchmark and  as one recent survey suggests  may prevent nearly 1 9 million adverse drug events and reduce federal spending by approximately  29 billion over the next 10 years   Under Sununus bill  for each electronic prescription written by a physician from January 1  2008 through December 31  2011  the Medicare Part D prescription drug plan would pay the physician an extra  1 minus the percentage of total electronic prescriptions which the drug plan would recoup by reducing the pharmacy dispensing fee by the same amount  The legislation would direct the HHS Secretary to update the formula and estimates every 6 months in order to promote additional e script usage   Sununu explained that his legislation  first introduced in the 109th Congress  would harness a proven technological advancement to benefit patients and pharmacies alike  According to the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services  30  of prescriptions require call backs for clarification  resulting in over 800 million prescription related telephone calls annually  By encouraging the widespread use of electronic prescriptions  patients  and pharmacies  stand to benefit from efficiencies that deliver long term cost savings and more accurately filled
