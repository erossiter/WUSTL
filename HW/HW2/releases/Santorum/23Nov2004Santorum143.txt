SENS. SANTORUM, SPECTER ANNOUNCE FEDERAL FUNDING FOR SOUTHEAST PENNSYLVANIA EDUCATION PROJECTS
  Sen. Rick Santorum (R-PA), Chairman of the Senate Republican Conference, and Sen. Arlen Specter (R-PA), Chairman of the Senate Appropriations Subcommittee on Labor, Health and Human Services, and Education, announced federal funding for projects in southeastern Pennsylvania, contained in the Fiscal Year 2005 Omnibus Appropriations bill.  
   "This funding will help to maintain and increase the high standards set in education throughout southeastern Pennsylvania," said Senator Santorum. "The world of education is moving forward at a rapid pace with new technology being introduced into classrooms virtually everyday. With funding like this, the goal is to provide all Pennsylvanians with a modern, quality education." 
   "I am pleased my colleagues have approved funding for these important southeastern Pennsylvania projects," Senator Specter said. "Education is the single greatest capital investment we can make in our nation's future, and this funding will provide resources that will empower students to become more productive members of society, live happier lives and make incredible contributions to our country." 
   The Fiscal Year 2005 Omnibus Appropriations Bill was passed by the Senate on November 20, 2004 and is pending final approval by the House of Representatives, followed by the President's signature. 
   Projects to receive funding include: 
   * $750,000 to the SouthEastern Pennsylvania Consortium for Higher Education to support the Institute of Mathematics and Science, which provides professional development to K-12 teachers on the use of emerging technologies and methodologies in the teaching of math and science. 
   * $650,000 to the American Foundation for Negro Affairs National Education and Research Fund in Philadelphia to provide supplementary education for minority students in grades 7 through 12 to raise their levels of achievement and to foster the goals of increasing minority access to higher education in medicine, law, and engineering. 
   * $500,000 to Philadelphia University in Philadelphia to support the next phase of the Technology Based Careers Initiative. The additional capacity to integrate technology into coursework and all program areas will allow the University to provide students with a technology-based education and prepare them for careers in business, science, technical design, industry and allied health. 
   * $500,000 to the National Liberty Museum in Philadelphia to expand its pilot Teacher Training program and to continue partnerships with community organizations and schools serving at-risk youth. 
   * $250,000 to the Philadelphia Martin Luther King, Jr. Association for Nonviolence, Inc. in Philadelphia to expand the College for Teens program which seeks to expose low-income, minority youth to higher education opportunities. The objective of the program is to introduce students to a college campus environment, give the students a solid foundation in core academic materials for the upcoming year, enhance current skills, and increase the potential of academic success. 
   * $250,000 to Project Rainbow in Philadelphia to provide early childhood and after school programs in an effort to increase educational opportunities and address the root causes of homelessness. Project Rainbow offers mentoring and tutoring services through its after school program to provide students with homework assistance. 
   * $250,000 to the Philadelphia Zoo in Philadelphia to support the development and implementation of a new science and environmental education curriculum for home-based students, using the Zoo as a non-traditional, living classroom. This funding will also support the Philadelphia Zoo's apprentice program. 
   * $200,000 to the University of Pennsylvania in Philadelphia to support the West Philadelphia Partnership for Community Schools, which is a national, community school model. K-12 campuses in West Philadelphia will operate comprehensive community school programs engaging children, parents, and community members in educational and cultural programs, recreation, job training, community improvement, and service activities on a year-round basis. Funds will support the development of curriculum, an after school program and professional development for teachers in the West Philadelphia higher education-assisted community schools. 
   * $200,000 to Cabrini College in Radnor to create a Center for Science, Education and Technology to provide practical, hands-on training for all students in math, basic science, and technology. The Center's facilities will allow for greater collaboration and professional development for area K-12 teachers. 
   * $200,000 to Temple University in Philadelphia for the Temple University Partnership Schools initiative to address student achievement in literacy and mathematics. The initiative will train teachers, principals and staff in high-poverty schools in reading and math instruction through graduate-level coursework, school-based training sessions, in-class coaching and leadership mentoring. Temple University Partnership Schools are a model for university involvement to public school reform. 
   * $150,000 to the Children's Literacy Initiative in Philadelphia to expand programs to improve the reading readiness and early literacy of children in high-poverty communities. The Children's Literacy Initiative offers professional development, individual coaching sessions in the classroom, and group workshops. 
   * $100,000 to MicroSociety, Inc. in Philadelphia to expand and develop Microsociety's curriculum, program, and materials. Microsociety will market and distribute its whole school reform model throughout PA and provide adequate training to administrators, teachers, and trainers. Microsociety creates a model of the real world to teach students. 
   * $100,000 to the National Museum of American Jewish History in Philadelphia to develop an interactive learning center linked to their web site that will extend the reach of the museum. This project is part of the Museum's effort to remain connected to and expand upon its audience while undergoing a capital campaign project that will close the facility from November 2004-July 2006. The museum will also increase its technological and multi-media capacities
