                                                                         News Room News Releases          Bill Is Now Sent to the President for Signature                      Washington  D C    Tuesday  March 7  2006     Senator Arlen Specter  Chairman of the Senate Judiciary Committee  today congratulated the House of Representatives for passing H R  32  the Stop Counterfeiting in Manufactured Goods Act  bi partisan legislation which provides heightened criminal penalties for trafficking counterfeit marks   The legislation  which passed the Senate in February of this year  will now be sent to the President for his signature     The legislation contains the language of S  1699  its Senate companion  that Senator Arlen Specter sponsored and shepherded through the Senate in November of last year     H R  32 amends Title 18 of the United States Code to close the loophole in the criminal trademark infringement statute  which currently does not criminally prohibit the trafficking of labels  patches  and stickers  and other counterfeit marks   The legislation would also ensure that counterfeit goods and marks seized in violation of this statute are properly disposed of and do not make their way back onto the street     Each year  counterfeiting costs the United States ten of thousands of jobs and millions of dollars in tax revenues     Senator Specter said     This legislation will help to protect the American economy from these counterfeit materials including potentially hazardous items such as automobile parts  batteries  and electrical equipment tagged with counterfeit labels       The current loophole was created in large part by the Tenth Circuit  s opinion in United States v  Giles  213 F 3d 1247  10th Cir  2000    In this case  the United States prosecuted the defendant for manufacturing and selling counterfeit Dooney   Bourke labels that third parties could later affix to generic purses   Examining Title 18  section 2320  of the United States Code  the Tenth Circuit held that persons who sell counterfeit trademarks that are not actually attached to any   goods or services    do not violate the federal criminal trademark infringement statute   The Bureau of Customs and Border Protection estimates that trafficking in counterfeit goods costs the United States in excess  200 million annually     This legislation was cosponsored by
