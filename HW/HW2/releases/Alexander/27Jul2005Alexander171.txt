                                                                                                                   Alexander Honors Historically Black Colleges and Universities Cosponsors bill to designate   National Historically Black Colleges and Universities Week          July 27th  2005   WASHINGTON    U S  Sen  Lamar Alexander  R TN  has joined Sen  Lindsey Graham  R SC  in cosponsoring S RES 158 asking the president to designate the week beginning September 11  2005 as  National Historically Black Colleges and Universities Week       I  m proud to join Senator Graham in honoring the accomplishments of the 105 Historically Black Colleges and Universities across the country  especially in recognizing the six schools in Tennessee  Fisk University  Knoxville College  Lane College  Lemoyne Owen College  Meharry Medical College and Tennessee State University have not only provided an outstanding education for thousands of students  they have also provided a great deal of leadership across Tennessee  My friend  the late Alex Haley  used to say    Find the good and praise it    I  m proud to be praising these colleges and universities on their good work       According to the U S  Department of Education  Historically Black Colleges and Universities  HBCUs  enroll 14 percent of all African American students in higher education  In 1999  these institutions matriculated 24 percent of all African American students enrolled in four year colleges  awarded masters degrees and first professional degrees to about one in six African American men and women  and awarded 24 percent of all baccalaureate degrees earned by African Americans nationwide   There are 105 HBCUs in the United States  including 40 public four year  11 public two year  49 private four year  and 5 private 2 year institutions  Most are more than 100 years old with Cheyney University of Pennsylvania  founded in 1837  being the oldest of these institutions  Tennessee is home to Fisk University  Knoxville College  Lane College  Lemoyne Owen College  Meharry Medical College  and Tennessee State University    An HBCU  as defined by the Higher Education Act of 1965  is     any historically black college or university that was established prior to 1964  whose principal mission was  and is  the education of black Americans  and that is accredited by a nationally recognized accrediting agency or association determined by the Secretary  of Education  to be a reliable authority as to the quality of training offered or is  according to such an agency or association  making reasonable progress toward accreditation     Alexander also cosponsored S RES 422 asking the president to designate a  National Historically Black Colleges and Universities Week  in 2004
